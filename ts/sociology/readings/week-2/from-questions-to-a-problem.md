### Introduction
- Identify the significance of your research question with 3 steps:
  1. I am studying `[topic]`;
  2. because I want to find out what/why/how `[question]`;
  3. to help my reader understand `[significance]`.
- Significance may begin as personal, but eventually, you must state the significance to the reader.

### Understanding Research Problems
- To make your research matter, you must address a problem that your community also wants to solve.

#### Practical Problems: What Should We Do?
- Everyday research begins not with dreaming up a topic but thinking about a practical problem that cannot be ignored.
- A research problem is defined by what you do not know about the practical problem.
- A practical problem is caused by some conditions in the world.
- To address it, someone needs to first understand it.

#### Conceptual Problems: What Should We Think?
- The need for knowledge or understanding raises a conceptual problem.
- Arises when we do not understand something in the world as well as we would like.
- Relationship between practical and conceptual/research problems:

```
[Practical Problem] motivates [Research Question] defines [Coneptual Research] leads to [Research Answer] helps to solve [Practical Problem]
```

- You need to think about a specific goal and problem.

### Understanding the Common Structure of Problems
- Practical and conceptual problems alike have the same two-part structure:
  - a situation or *condition*
  - *consequences* caused by that condition

#### The Nature of Practical Problems
- A practical problem consists of a condition and costs.
- We acknowledge a problem only when we stop asking *So what?* and instead say *What do we do about it?*

#### The Nature of Conceptual Problems
- Conceptual problem: the condition is not knowing or understanding something.
- Questions are valuable in forcing you to state what you don't know.
- The consequence of conceptual problems is ignorance.
- Make clear through *So what?*/*What do we do about it?* cycles the relevance and significance of your contribution to the reader.

<br>

<br>

### Distinguishing Pure and Applied Research
- *Pure* research - addresses a conceptual problem that does not bear directly on any practical situation.
- *Applied* research - addresses a conceptual problem that does have practical consequences.

#### Connecting Research to Practical Consequences
- To formulate a good applied research project, show how the answer to an indirect question plausibly helps answer the following indirect question.
- When stating the problem in the introduction, present it as a purely conceptual research problem; wait until the conclusion to suggest its practical application.

### Finding a Good Research Problem
- Researchers often begin a project without being clear about what their real problem is.

#### Ask for Help
- Why would anyone want an answer to your question?
- What would they do with it?
- What new questions might an answer raise?

#### Look for Problems as You Read
- Are there contradictions, inconsistencies to be explained?
- Make sure the disconnect does not stem from your misreading.

<br>

<br>

### Learning to Work with Problems
- The first question is not *Can I solve it?* but *Will readers think it should be solved?*
- Formulate a question worth answering.
