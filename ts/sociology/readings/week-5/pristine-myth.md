### Abstract
The narrative that in 1492 the Americas were sparsely populated is false. The Native American landscape was humanized everywhere.

<br>

---

<br>

### Introduction
- The landscape Columbus encountered in the 16th century was no pristine, virgin, wilderness.
- Romanticist and primitivist writers have painted the wilderness in a particular virgin light.
- Indian populations in the Americas have been substantial
- Kirkpatrick Sale in 1990 asserts that Europeans transformed nature.
- The impact of Native Americans is neither localized nor ephemeral; they changed the landscape everywhere.
- By 1492, Indian activity had modified forest extent, created new grasslands, formed agricultural fields, houses, towns, roads, and trails.
- The Indian landscape of 1492 had largely vanished because of the demise of the native population.

<br>

---

<br>

### Indian Numbers
- The size of the native population is important; many Indians were living in the Americas before 1492.
- Did Las Casas exaggerate the numbers? The New World population lies likely between 43 and 65 percent.
- The native population had been growing, but European entry reversed this trend.
  - Populations fell from Old World disease.

<br>

---

<br>

### Vegetation
- Forests had been disturbed by Indian activity before European occupation.
  - Ground fires, agricultural clearing, and burning.
  - Indian burning could account for vegetation changes.
- Indian clearing and burning created many grasslands and the "prairie belt".
- Intended fires had different vegetation impacts than natural fires.
- Burning encouraged fire-tolerant and sun-loving species.
- Indians expanded into pockets of grasslands, advancing grasslands.
- Savannas have been cut out of the rainforest and maintained by burning.
- The tropical rain forest has a reputation of being pristine, but the forests are largely the result of anthropogenic actions.
  - Indian burning, swiddens, and manipulation of composition have modified the tropical forest.
    - Charcoal is very common in the Amazonian soil.
- To what extent does the evidence reflect Indian burning in contrast to natural fires?
- About 40% of the tropical forest is secondary as a result of human clearing.
- Many Latin American forests are also the result of humanization: the kinds, numbers, and distributions of useful species are managed by human populations.
  - Planting, transplanting, sparing, protection of wild plants.
  - DIspersal of edible plant seeds via defecation and spitting.
- "Cultural" and "anthropogenic" forests in Amazonia: specialized forests.

<br>

<br>

### Wildlife
- There is debate over the indigenous impact on wildlife.
- Dense populations of Central Mexico had already reduced the number of the large game when Cortez arrived in 1519.
- Forest disturbance also increased herbaceous forage.

<br>

---

<br>

### Agriculture
- Native Americans altered the landscape with cultivated fields
- Many Indian fields were large in their expanse.
- The size of native populations caused deforestation and severe land degradation.
- Severe soil erosion was widespread, rather than just the result of European plowing and deforestation.
- Under Spanish rule, the landscape in Mexico did not recover.

<br>

---

<br>

### The Built Landscape
- The Spaniards and other Europeans were impressed by large and flourishing Indian cities.
- The abandonment of houses and villages quickly obscured once-populated sites.
- Mounds of different shapes and sizes were constructed for temples, burials, settlements, and so on.
- Extensive travel routes were needed; however, many do not remain.

<br>

---

<br>

### Recovery
- Case for significant environmental recovery was the result of Indian population decline.
- The pace of forest regeneration varied across the New World.
- Wildlife partially recovered in Eastern North America with reduced hunting pressure, but commercial hunting continued.
- Indian fields were replaced with European livestock and sugar plantations.

<br>

---

<br>

### Conclusions
- By 1492, Indian activity had already modified vegetation and wildlife, caused erosion, and created earthworks and settlements.
- The pristine myth does not begin with Columbus; "Paradise" was a humanized paradise.
- Early observers were unaware of human impacts that are obvious today.
- The human impact is not a process of increasing change or degradation; it is interrupted by periods of reversal and rehabilitation.
  - Cultural collapse, population decline, etc. change that environment.
- Change is the only constant.
