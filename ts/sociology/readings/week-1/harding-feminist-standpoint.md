### Summary and Key Points
- **Standpoint theory** argues that research should begin with and place control in the *researched* subject, rather than the *researcher*.
- When researchers choose which hypotheses are interesting to the discipline and test them in discipline-immersed methods, they leave the values and interests of the field untouched.
- A core tenet of the scientific ideology is to be value-neutral and "objective".
  - Researchers immersed in an institution cannot recognize the values that institution propagates.
  - *The standpoint of those outside the institution is required in order to critically examine the objectivity of that institution.*
  - Certain "good" values are beneficial for research; thus, standpoint theory attempts to make the standard of objectivity stronger. Politics is required to identify values and interests in institutions.
- The colonial character of the research process - in "conquering" the researched and painting a certain portrait of them - can be diminished by imposing stronger, standpoint theory-based standards of objectivity.

### Origins
- Feminist standpoint theories originated with women's political movements in the 1960s-1970s.
- Dorothy Smith and Nancy Hartsock argue that *existing relationships between politics and knowledge were far from the socially implemented and acceptable progressive ideas*.
  - Prevailing philosophies of science and research projects were *politically regressive*.
- Many clusters of essays debate the strengths and limitations of methodologies in journals.
- The standpoint "logic of research" in each other social justice movement's knowledge production is recognized.
- Evidence for standpoint claims appeared in the documentation of sexist results of biological and social research.
- **Gaps between actual knowledge and those desired by women's movements.**
- Feminist standpoint theory drew upon the proletarian standpoint of Marx and Engels.
  - Only by analyzing the standpoint of the worker could the system be analyzed.
  - Fredric Jameson: only the feminist theorists could fulfill the fruitful logic of Marxist arguments.
- **"Logic of inquiry"** - resources provided by the "stranger's" social position. Development of "outside within" position of disciplinary researchers.
  - A researcher can learn deeper social relationships.
- Standpoint logic is the structure of most recent multicultural and postcolonial studies.
  - Western natural sciences are *local knowledge systems*, coproduced by social relations.
  - The coproductionist argument acknowledges that Western sciences are more powerful.
- What is the abandoned "logic of inquiry" adopted by feminist standpoint theorists?

### A Standpoint Logic of Inquiry
- Feminist theories argue that sexist understandings of gender relations appeared in abstract and obvious ways.
  - Social relations between women and men resemble the worker-boss relationship.
  - Exploitation, domination, and oppression were legitimated through conceptual frameworks of research disciplines and social institutions they served.
  - Thus, there are parallel structures in gender and class relations.
- Feminist researchers were participants in emerging women's movements.
  - Provided accounts of articulations of gender relations.
  - Began research as a concern of *everyday life* rather than in institutions and disciplines.
- *Moral theory was not attentive to the moral decisions made by women* - rather, those made by men.
- Natural and social science disciplines *could not critically examine how their conceptual frameworks bolstered power in society*.
- Science holds that the "context of discovery" should be left free of methodological controls.
  - This blocks social values and interests into the research process.
  - Values and interests shared by the research community that is androcentric, white supremacist, and Eurocentric cannot be detected by research methods.
  - A community that excludes those outside the values could not be identified.
- This notion of objectivism legitimizes a notion of objectivity too narrow to allow for value-free research.
- "Context of justification" - hypotheses that are interesting to the discipline are tested in discipline-approved ways, leaving values and interests untouched.
- **In directing research to begin with dominant disciplinary conceptual frameworks and not the lives of oppressed peoples, the context of justification is furthered.**
  - Rather, begin with the lives of oppressed peoples and extend the benefits of methodological controls back to the beginning of the research, including the *"context of **discovery**"*.
- **Standpoint theory attempts to change how we derive which questions are worth pursuing**; it is not more "practical than theoretical", but starts from every day to reevaluate prevailing theory.
- *Objectivism conceptualizes value-neutrality of objectivity too broadly* - not all social values and interests have the same bad effects upon the results of research.
- Making explicit the "logic" of standpoint; substantive feminist research starting from women's lives *did* produce more empirically accurate accounts.


Themes in standpoint accounts to understand "illicit" research practice:

| Theme | Elaboration |
| --- | --- |
| The structuring of society has epistemological[^1] consequences. | Knowledge and power are linked; what people do enables and limits what they can know. Social structures provide a laboratory within which we can explore how different activities enable and block insights. |
| When material life is hierarchy organized, relations available to rulers and the rules will be opposed. | The dominant group's understanding will support the legitimacy of the dominating position, whereas the opposite is true for the dominated. |
| Oppressors' false perceptions are made real. | Hierarchal structures and institutions engage in conceptual practices that solidify and disseminate continued power. |
| It takes both science and politics to see beneath an oppressor's institutionalized vision. | No one's understanding can completely escape its historical moment, yet the success of standpoint research only requires a degree of freedom from a dominant understanding. |
| The achievement of standpoint brings the possibility of liberation. | An oppressed group must become a group for itself to see the importance of engaging in political and scientific struggles. Group consciousness enables struggles and feminist perceptions. |

- **Standpoint claim**: political struggles are necessary to reveal institutional practices; they are systematically knowledge-producing.
- Standpoint theory is part of post-Marxian critical theories that require ideological critique as **crucial to the growth of knowledge**.

### Against Intrinsically Colonial Research Processes
- Researchers often stand from a higher social status than those they study.
  - This structurally designated relation between the researcher and the researched is intrinsically socially unequal - a "colonial" relationship.
- It is the *researcher* - influenced by their discipline and culture - who decides on the conditions, peoples, events, and processes the research project focuses on.
  - The researched area allotted little say in this process.
  - The research process belongs entirely to the researcher, and could not be sufficiently objective.
- Emancipatory movements have **two reasons** to criticize researcher control of research.
  - *Political*: behaviors of the powerful fund social research.
  - *Scientific*: the disempowerment of the researched nourishes distorted accounts of their beliefs and behaviors.
- Disempowerment of research subjects further disempowers them and produced "bad science" considered to be "good science".

### Futile Strategies
- Futility of many widespread strategies requires recognition.
  - Social statuses the researcher and research bring to research processes are immutable.
  - Fundamental economic, political, and social structural inequalities that position the researcher and the researched will not be changed by the research process alone.
- Researchers "confessing" their high social status to the reader is another unsuccessful strategy.
  - The reader still must figure out how the researcher's location shaped their work.
  - Furthermore, assumes individualistic liberalism: individuals can identify all relevant cultural assumptions.
- Forgoing theoretical or conceptual input is also futile.
  - Researchers often attempt to "record the voices" of their subjects.
  - This restricts the researcher to a transcription machine.
- How does standpoint theory block colonial relations of social research? In three sites of research:
  1. Selection and design of the research project: "context of discovery".
  2. Conduct research.
  3. Communication, interpretation, the theorization of data.

### The Context of Discovery: Whose Questions?
- Standpoint approaches recommend that the "context of discovery" be brought under *methodological controls*.
- Dominant group's values and interests shape research projects at this stage of inquiry.
- What are politically and scientifically valuable problems to pursue in research?
- By seeking out standpoints from the lives of oppressed people, the views of funders and sponsors of research can be balanced.
  - For example, feminist researchers are both trained in professional communities and are members of an oppressed group - thus, they must be in a particularly good position to locate critical standpoints.
- **How to identify useful standpoints?** Standpoint methods are *politically engaged* - they are not "value-free".
  - Standpoint methods recognize that some values advance the growth of knowledge, and others block it.

### Strong Objectivity
- There is no single, fixed meaning of "objectivity".
- Objectivity is attributed to methods of research.
- Maximizing objectivity is equated with maximizing social/value neutrality.
  - Good methods identify social values and interests behind the research process.
  - When social values and interests are held by all the researchers in a field, they cannot be identified.
  - Starting research from outside a field can enable the detection of dominant values.
  - Standpoint theory enables us to see the narrowness of conventional objectivity.
- Standpoint theorists have argued that some values can be productive to knowledge, in contrast with the value-neutrality requirement of traditional objectivity.
- "Strong objectivity" is called for by standpoint approaches to replace weak "objectivist" notions.
- The colonial character of the research process can be diminished by requiring stronger standards for objectivity.

### Responses to Criticism
- Standpoint theory has drawn criticism from rationalists, empiricists, and positivists.
- We need philosophies of research that enable the growth of today's knowledge, not institutions of feudalism, monarchy, etc.
- Do standpoint methodologies automatically privilege "women's ways of knowing"?
  - Experience is not public knowledge; standpoint requires both collective intellectual and political struggles.
  - Feminist development of standpoint theory changes how we think about the collective experience in the production of knowledge.
- Standpoint projects argue for a "principled relativism", rather than a damaging epistemological relativism.
  - Scientific knowledge is always constituted in sponsoring society.
  - Standpoint theory demands stronger standards for empirical reliability.
- Standpoint writings do not ignore important differences in the conditions of women across social and cultural contexts.
- Standpoint theory is useful for the natural sciences, although it was developed primarily with a focus on social research.

### Transformations
Four important transformations in the emergence of standpoint methodology from the feminist writings of the 1970s-1980s.

| Transformation | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Dissemination of feminist writings has vastly increased in both social and natural science research. } Standpoint methodologies are widely used in all social science disciplines, as well as in biology and the natural sciences. "Logic of research" is recognized as a central feature of knowledge projects in every social justice movement. |
| Controversiality has expanded with the use of these methodologies. | Standpoint theory engages with fundamental anxieties confronting states and cultures. Standpoint theory will not lose its controversial character as long as inequalities persist. |
| Standpoint methodologies take on their own distinctive "local" characters. | Standpoint methodologies or epistemologies should be referred to in the plural, as they shared a "logic of research". |
| Members of dominant social groups are important in advancing standpoint knowledge. | Researchers routinely ask others to give them feedback, regardless of their experiences. |

### Conclusion
- Standpoint theories have made intellectual use of economic, social, psychological, and cultural inequalities.
- Investigating important questions to disadvantaged groups enables them to gain greater control over their conditions.
- Standpoint epistemology can help move towards a genuinely democratic society with no inequality.
- Standpoint theory has not only survived but flourished amidst controversy.
