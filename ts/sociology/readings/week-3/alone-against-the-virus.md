### Introduction
- Decades of neoliberal neglect have made it more difficult to fight COVID-19.
- There needs to be a "New Deal for public health".

<br>

---

<br>

### Profit-driven health care makes the virus harder to manage
- The US response has been delayed by failures in testing the virus.
- Those hardest hit are the most vulnerable; we have no natural immunity to the virus.
- What social immunity can we built in a "body politic that has been ravaged for decades by neoliberal policies?"
- A profit-driven health care system, social dislocation, crisis of social reproduction.
  - These conditions make the virus harder to manage.
- Inequality is associated with poorer health outcomes.
- Epidemics are great levelers: they collapse social classes, even if people at the bottom get the worst of it.

<br>

<br>

### Americans demand a new politics of care
- The question is if we can learn something about the coronavirus.
- It is not just infections but all sorts of health that feed on the weakness of our current system.
- A majority of Americans see universal healthcare as a core responsibility.
- Social mobilization is a key tool for achieving these goals.
- Many features that make COVID-19 disruptive are deeper roots in the political and social order.
- Universal health care in the US has been called un-American.
- Efforts to expand health coverage have run into white supremacy and racism.
- Hospitals and the pharmaceutical industry are always in for a decentralized and weak system.
- Conservatives and insurers have blocked what most industrialized countries have had for decades; "we lack... [a] proper... health care system".
- A healthcare system that has a focus on maximizing profit has given us fewer beds per capita and a glut of overpriced specialists.
- One in five Americans cannot pay their monthly bills fully.
- 27.5 million people are uninsured.

<br>

<br>

### Public health institutions have been weakened
- Fragmented health infrastructure means that it is more likely to be inundated with waves of sickness.
- People who cannot get early care will get sicker; more infectious people in our communities further spreading the virus rather than being treated in facilities.
- A patchwork of a healthcare system that purposefully disadvantages poor communities.
- Implications of austerity budgets of recent years are being felt; fiscal policy is a bipartisan folly.
- Funding for the CDC has decreased by 10%; seventeen states have cut their health budgets in the past few years. 
- The CDC's Public Health Emergency Preparedness program has been cut by a third.
- Public health programs have suffered under both parties.
- Treatments could have a more immediate impact, but at what cost?
- Drug prices have spiraled out of control because the US has no systematic means to control them.

<br>

<br>

### Envisioning a fairer coronavirus response
- Medicare for All component as a new infrastructure of care.
- Examine a critical mass of people in the position to follow important measures.
- Employment in the country systematically marginalizes our ability to care for ourselves and others.
- The risk is extraordinary for those caring for others. Their risk is our risk too.
- Temporary measures will help, but broader social support needed is long-term: addressing chronic disease in America.
- The United States has shunted millions out of the circle of care; these are the most vulnerable in the epidemic.
