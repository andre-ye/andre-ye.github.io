### Introduction
- If you have a question and at least one promising answer, you can start testing it with data.
- To test your hypothesis, you need a plan.

<br>

---

<br>

### Knowing How to Use Three Kinds of Sources

#### Primary Sources
- Provide "raw data" you can test your hypothesis on.
- A research paper is rarely written without using primary sources.

#### Secondary Sources
- Research reports that use primary data to solve research problems.
- Written for scholarly and professional audiences.
- Use secondary source data only if you cannot find primary source data.

#### Tertiary Sources
- Synthesize and report on secondary sources for general readers.
- Use tertiary sources to get a feel for a topic.

<br>

### Locating Sources Through a Library
- Today's libraries connect you to the best online resources.
- Library of Congress and state university databases and online catalogs are useful.

#### Planning Your Search
- *Plan your search* - begins with an overview of the research on your topic.
- Talk to librarians; you will save time if you prepare questions.
- Talk to experts in the field; begin with teachers and advisers.
- Consult general reference works.
- Consult specialized reference works.

#### Searching for Specific Searches
- Search your library catalog.
- Research in a neglected field might make you famous, but it won't work for a paper due in a few weeks.
- Use online databases.
- Prowl the stacks of the library.

#### Locating Sources on the Internet
- The Internet's strength in numbers is also its limitation.
- Use the Internet freely only if it's a primary source.

### Evaluating Sources for Relevance and Reliability

#### Evaluating Sources for Relevance

| Source Type | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| Book |  Skim index, skim pages, skim first and last paragraphs in chapters, skim intros and summary chapters, skim editor's introduction, check bibliography. |
| Article | Read abstract, skim section headings, skim intro and conclusion. |
| Online | Follow steps to a journal article; skim major sections; check for keywords in site map. |

#### Evaluating Sources for Reliability
1. Is the source published or posted by a reputable press?
2. Was the book or article peer-reviewed? (Few commercial magazines use peer review; many essay collections are only reviewed by named editors.)
3. Is the author a reputable scholar?
4. If the source is available only online, is it sponsored by a reputable organization?
5. Is the source current?
6. Does the source notes and a bibliography / bibliographical data?
7. Does the source approach the topic judiciously?
8. If the source is a book, has it been well-reviewed?
10. Has the source been frequently cited by others?

### Following Bibliographical Trails
- Most sources give you *trailheads* for bibliographical searches.
- When you find a useful book, skim the bibliography and works cited.
- Journal articles begin with a  review of previous research.
- One source always leads to others, which leads to others...

### Use People as Primary Sources
- In some areas, you have to collect primary data from people.
- They may provide useful information.
- Don't ignore people in local business, government, or civic organizations.
