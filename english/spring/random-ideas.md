### Silent Spring Discussion on Chapters 1 and 2
- Before, she displays the impact of nature on man. Strange blight, stillness. Part of the fairy tale or the fable is the lack of human agency we percieve here. And humans are being affected; maladies in the chickens, sure, but the farmers are ill; the children suddenly die. Ultimately, we are affected as humans by this strange force with which we have no control over. It is witchcraft, it is enemy action.
- Now, she shows the impact of man on nature to illustrate the reciprocity and full-circularity of our actions. And the remainder of the book is not on a unidirectional relationship between man and nature but of its cyclical relation.
- A discussion on how humans manage their power. one of my favorite and most thought-provoking chapters.
- There is no time. The way of the human is different than that of nature. Our scale is much smaller and we effectuate unsustainable change. We are disrupting a balance of nature. "But in the modern era, there is no time."
   - Already here, we have a fusion of science and of poeticism. Darwin and evolutionary theory.
- Moreover, she illustrates a resilience of nature. It is stronger than us; we may try to exercise our power in a futile attempt to conquer the land, but "The chemical war is never won." Nature will continue with or without us; it operates on a grander scale. Nature of agency
- I get the impression of perhaps willing negligence, of an immature child that has not fully developed the maturity and sensibility to understand their role in the wider world. Perhaps greed has something to do with this.
- Lastly, she makes an appeal to the public regarding the nature of knowledge. To understand how humanity decides to maneuver themselves in this wider world of the universe, we need to understand the full ciruclarity and reciprocity of our actions. We have an obligation to endure, and thus to know. That was incredibly convincing appeal.
