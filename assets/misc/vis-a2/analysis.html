Andre Ye, andreye@mit.edu

Subtheme: Speculation

The background readings keep returning to one question: Who is this city being built for? I use Boston's residential sales data to explore that, looking at the geography of prices, who's flipping properties, and who owns what.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How do prices and sales activity vary across Boston over time, and does the geography of expensive vs. affordable areas line up with what the Globe and MAPC describe?
  2. How common are flipping and entity buying, and where do they show up? MAPC's "Homes for Profit" report and the transfer-fee debate both focus on investor flips and corporate ownership, so I looked at where those concentrate geographically.
  3. Who's actually doing the flipping? Policy discussions often target "investors" broadly, but I wanted to check whether flippers are mostly corporate entities or ordinary individuals.
  4. Where does multi-property ownership concentrate? The Globe and the subtheme both raise concerns about absentee ownership ("safety-deposit boxes in the sky"), so I looked for proxies in the data.
Limits applied to all figures:
  • Residential: Only residential usecodes (1–4 family, condo, apartment, accessory, etc.). Commercial, industrial, vacant land, and other non-dwelling codes are dropped.
  • Price cap: Price-based figures cap at $5M so a handful of extremes (and likely errors above $50M) don't drive the scale; that drops about 3% of residential sales.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

The readings ask who this city is being built for. The data don't answer that directly, but they paint a suggestive picture: prices have risen across the board, flipping is real but moderate, and most of it is done by individuals rather than institutional or corporate buyers.

Median prices have climbed steadily, and both flipping and entity buying stepped up around 2010. Geographically, price growth has been broad. Back Bay, Downtown, and northern South Boston are consistently expensive, while the Fenway and southern South Boston are a bit lower but still high. There's no clean dividing line; expensive and more affordable pockets are mixed throughout the city. Flipping is similarly widespread, but many high-flip areas don't have heavy entity presence, which prompted a closer look at who's behind it.

The answer, mostly, is ordinary non-investor individuals. Only about 3.2% of non-investor purchases are flips, and those flippers buy at slightly higher prices than non-flippers, but the gap is modest. They look more like people with means than large-scale investors. The time-series view tells the same story: non-entities make up the majority of flips in every year. Multi-property ownership offers one more lens on absenteeism and clusters in the core and some suburbs. Overall, speculation in this data is real but modest, and it's driven more by wealthier individuals than by institutional or corporate players.
